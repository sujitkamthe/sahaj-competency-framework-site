Sahaj Engineering Competency Framework

Clarity without hierarchy. Impact over titles.

How to Read These Expectations

At Sahaj, we believe that great software engineering is inseparable from thinking, ownership, and human judgment. We are a technology consulting organisation, not a feature factory. Our work succeeds when we solve the right problems well, not merely when we deliver what is asked.

This competency framework exists to create clarity without hierarchy. We intentionally do not use formal titles or grades. Instead, we describe how impact evolves over time—from learning and contributing safely, to shaping direction, building trust, and growing others.

What We Value

Technical Excellence as a Baseline

Clean code, strong design, testing, and sound engineering practices are expected at every stage. Technical depth is necessary, but by itself it is not enough for long-term growth.

Ownership Over Output

We value people who take responsibility for outcomes—technical, delivery, client, and organisational—not just for completing tasks.

Consulting Mindset

Sahaj engineers are expected to think like consultants: to understand problems deeply, remove ambiguity, propose solutions, and help stakeholders make better decisions. Doing what is asked without questioning unclear or risky assumptions has a natural growth ceiling.

Delivery Excellence, Not Heroics

Sustainable, predictable delivery matters more than speed or firefighting. We optimize for long-term health of teams and systems.

People and Culture as First-Class

Growing others, building trust, and strengthening teams are not "extra" activities. They are core to how impact scales at Sahaj.

Transparency and Trust

In a culture with open conversations and open salaries, expectations must be explicit, observable, and fair. This framework is designed to make growth paths visible and to support honest self-assessment.

How to Use This Framework

  • This is not a checklist and not everyone will excel in all areas at once.
  • Expectations increase in depth and scope, not simply in quantity of work.
  • Growth happens by expanding impact across multiple dimensions over time.
  • Each section includes prompting questions to help with unbiased self-reflection, focused on outcomes rather than effort.

Most importantly, this framework is meant to support adult conversations—about growth, impact, and contribution—without hiding behind titles, years of experience, or vague notions of seniority.

Personas

These personas explain how Sahaj expects impact to evolve, not what someone is "allowed" to do. Growth is about expanding reach across capability areas. No single straight path. Different areas mature at different speeds.

0-1 Years

Explorer

Learning the terrain

"I am learning how things are done, why they are done this way, and how to contribute safely."
1-3 Years

Artisan

Mastering the craft

"I can deliver high-quality work independently and understand the context around it."

Sparking change & unblocking

"I actively improve outcomes by shaping solutions, influencing people, and unblocking systems."

Capability Areas

At Sahaj, expectations are described across five capability areas. These are best understood as dimensions of impact, not roles, grades, ratings, or levels. Capability areas are intentionally independent. Growth happens by expanding impact across multiple areas over time, not by maximising a single one.

This is not a scorecard. The radar shows where your impact currently shows up, not how good you are.

Technical Delivery

How well you build systems

Engineering craft, execution quality, and technical excellence. Building the right thing well, making appropriate trade-offs, and leaving systems healthier than you found them.

Consulting

How well you clarify problems and shape solutions

Understanding problems beyond tickets, removing ambiguity, reframing vague asks into solvable problem statements, and helping stakeholders make better decisions.

Delivery Excellence

How reliably and sustainably value is delivered

Predictability over heroics. Sustainable pace over short-term speed. Good delivery is a system property, not an individual trait.

Mentorship & Talent Growth

Who grows because of you

Coaching, knowledge sharing, feedback, creating psychologically safe environments, and building teams that are strong even in your absence.

Communication & Influence

How far your impact travels

Communicating clearly, facilitating discussions, building stakeholder relationships, and influencing without authority.

How the Capability Areas Work Together

No single capability area is sufficient on its own.

  • Strong Technical Delivery without Consulting leads to execution-only roles
  • Strong Consulting without Technical depth erodes trust
  • Strong Delivery without Mentorship creates bottlenecks
  • Strong Influence without substance creates noise

Sustainable growth at Sahaj comes from balanced expansion across capability areas over time, with different capability areas becoming more prominent at different stages.

How to Do Your Self-Assessment

This framework is designed to support honest reflection, not optimistic self-rating. The goal is to help you clearly identify where you operate today, not where you aspire to be.

You will choose one persona only and then assess how consistently you demonstrate that persona across capability areas.

1

Choose Your Current Persona (Single Choice)

Choose one persona that best describes how you operate most of the time, across your role.

Do not choose aspirationally.
Choose the persona whose behaviours you already demonstrate consistently and without prompting.

Personas (from early to advanced)

Explorer Learning the fundamentals, contributing with guidance
Artisan Reliable individual contributor with strong execution
Catalyst Expands impact beyond own work; enables teams
Multiplier Scales impact through people, systems, and decisions
Strategist Shapes direction, priorities, and long-term outcomes

It is expected that growth through these personas is sequential.

Ask yourself:

  • Where do I operate most of the time, not occasionally?
  • Where do others rely on me without prompting?
  • If my role stayed the same for 6 months, which persona would still describe me?

You must select exactly one persona.
You will not choose different personas for different capability areas.

2

Assess Yourself Against That Persona

Once you've chosen your persona, assess how well you demonstrate that persona's expectations across all relevant Capability Areas (Technical Delivery, Consulting, Delivery Excellence, Mentorship, Influence).

You are NOT asking:

"What is my level in this area?"

You ARE asking:

"How consistently do I demonstrate the behaviours expected of my chosen persona in this area?"

3

Gather Evidence (Not Effort)

For each Capability Area, list concrete evidence that shows you operating at your chosen persona level.

Focus on:

  • Outcomes you owned
  • Decisions you influenced
  • Problems that stopped recurring because of you
  • People who grew or succeeded because of your involvement

Avoid:

  • Intent ("I try to...", "I aim to...")
  • Effort ("I worked hard...", "I spent many hours...")
  • One-off heroics or exceptional circumstances

If you struggle to find evidence, that is a signal, not a failure.

4

Rate Yourself Honestly (Per Capability Area)

Using the definitions below, rate yourself for each Capability Area, relative to your chosen persona.

Below Expectations

  • You demonstrate some behaviours of the persona, but not most
  • Impact is inconsistent, fragile, or dependent on others
  • Outcomes rely on favourable conditions
  • You often need reminders, escalation, or rescue
Guiding questions:
  • Would this area stall without support?
  • Do others compensate for gaps I haven't closed yet?

Meets Expectations

  • You demonstrate most behaviours consistently
  • Impact is reliable and repeatable
  • Others trust you to own this area without supervision
  • You deliver outcomes across different contexts
Guiding questions:
  • Would people confidently depend on me here?
  • Does this show up beyond one project or team?

Exceeds Expectations

  • You raise the bar for this area at your persona level
  • Others learn by watching or working with you
  • Your impact extends beyond your immediate scope
  • Problems stop recurring due to structural improvements you introduced
Guiding questions:
  • Are others changing how they work because of me?
  • Would this area noticeably weaken if I stepped away?
5

Look for Imbalance

Your persona choice should be supported across capability areas.

Key principles:

  • Strong Technical Delivery is mandatory, but insufficient for growth
  • Exceeding expectations in one area does not compensate for being below in another
  • Persistent gaps signal a growth ceiling, regardless of strengths elsewhere

Examples:

Strong Technical Delivery + weak Consulting Catalyst ceiling
Strong Execution + weak Mentorship Multiplier ceiling
Strong Influence + weak Delivery Excellence Credibility risk

Growth requires broadening impact, not maximising a single strength.

6

Validate Externally

Before finalising your assessment:

Ask peers:

  • "Where do you see my strongest impact?"
  • "Where do you rely on me without needing follow-ups?"

Ask leads:

  • "Where do I consistently operate without support?"
  • "Where would my absence be felt most?"

Look for patterns, not isolated comments.

If your self-assessment is meaningfully higher than the feedback, trust the feedback.

Explicit Growth Principle

Strong Technical Delivery is mandatory but not sufficient.

Growth beyond Artisan requires:

  • Consulting
  • Delivery Excellence

Growth beyond Catalyst requires:

  • Mentorship
  • Influence
  • System-level thinking

Persona advancement reflects how far your impact travels, not how good you are at a single thing.

Persona Anti-Patterns

Common self-overrating traps. Use this section to stress-test your persona choice. If multiple anti-patterns apply strongly to you, you are likely rating yourself one persona too high.

Explorer "Learning ≠ Operating"

Common traps when rating yourself too high:

  • Confusing exposure with competence ("I've seen this before")
  • Relying on step-by-step guidance but framing it as independence
  • Completing tasks only when scope is tightly defined by others
  • Needing frequent clarification but not recognising it as dependency
  • Delivering outcomes that work only in the exact same conditions

Red flags:

  • Work stalls without close support
  • You struggle to explain why something works
  • You need rework when conditions change slightly
→ If this applies, you are still Explorer, not Artisan.
Artisan "Strong Execution ≠ Expanded Impact"

Common traps when rating yourself too high:

  • Mistaking high-quality delivery for broader ownership
  • Being the "go-to person" because of availability, not leverage
  • Solving the same problems repeatedly without eliminating root causes
  • Waiting for direction, even when you could lead
  • Equating seniority or tenure with Catalyst behaviour

Red flags:

  • Impact is limited to your own tasks or tickets
  • You execute well but rarely shape the approach
  • Teams depend on you to do, not to enable
→ If this applies, you are Artisan, not Catalyst.
Catalyst "Helping ≠ Enabling at Scale"

Common traps when rating yourself too high:

  • Acting as a human bridge instead of fixing broken systems
  • Being involved in many things, but owning few outcomes end-to-end
  • Giving advice that is helpful but not adopted consistently
  • Creating momentum that depends on your constant presence
  • Confusing visibility with leverage

Red flags:

  • Things slow down when you step away
  • Others still rely on you to connect dots
  • Improvements don't survive beyond the project
→ If this applies, you are Catalyst, not Multiplier.
Multiplier "Delegation ≠ Multiplication"

Common traps when rating yourself too high:

  • Delegating tasks without transferring context or judgment
  • Mentoring individuals but not improving team capability overall
  • Scaling yourself by adding meetings instead of removing friction
  • Driving outcomes through personal authority rather than systems
  • Confusing org-level exposure with org-level impact

Red flags:

  • Quality drops sharply without your oversight
  • Teams execute because of you, not independently
  • Success is fragile and person-dependent
→ If this applies, you are Multiplier, not Strategist.
Strategist "Direction Setting ≠ Durable Strategy"

Common traps when rating yourself too high:

  • Producing narratives without changing execution reality
  • Defining vision without aligning incentives or constraints
  • Relying on charisma instead of structural clarity
  • Making decisions that sound right but don't survive trade-offs
  • Assuming long-term thinking without measurable outcomes

Red flags:

  • Strategy exists mostly in decks or conversations
  • Teams interpret direction differently
  • Results lag despite clear messaging
→ If this applies, you may still be Multiplier, not Strategist.

Universal Over-Rating Signals (All Personas)

If you notice these patterns, pause and reassess:

"I could do it if I were given the chance"
"I do this informally, just not officially"
"It worked once, so I count it"
"People come to me a lot" (without evidence of sustained change)
"I'm already doing parts of the next persona"

Reminder: Occasional behaviour does not define your persona.
Consistency, durability, and reliance without prompting do.

Final Self-Check

Before finalising your persona, ask:

  • If I stepped away for 3 months, what would break vs persist?
  • Is my impact person-dependent or system-dependent?
  • Would others describe me at this persona level without hesitation?

If the answer feels defensively justified rather than obvious, you're likely one level too high.
